MIB Cars Analysis

Commercial prioritization and forecasting from sales opportunity data

Strategic Analysis Group

Executive Summary

  • This analysis examines an auto-parts sales opportunity pipeline, identifying structural inefficiencies (i.e. CRM input errors, lack of prioritization, and inaccurate forecasting) that drive a structurally low 22.7% conversion rate.
  • We propose two static / at-creation models: a win/loss classifier (AUROC 73.6%) for early opportunity prioritization, and a regression model for deal sizing with 63.0% MdAPE on held-out test data.
  • We show that focusing on the top 20% of most likely opportunities lifts win rates from the 22.7% baseline to 51.7%; while focusing on the top 25% expected-value ranked opportunities captures 55.6% of total won revenue.
  • We recommend embedding scores in the CRM at opportunity creation, piloting via A/B test, and setting clear thresholds to operationalize prioritization in sales workflows.
  • We also simulate strategic levers under the static setup and find only modest upside, which reinforces that better process-history data is needed before using the models for bigger operating decisions.

Business framing

The pipeline is non-linear, so the data reflects a moving sales process

  • The current sales process tracks auto-parts opportunities across both new leads and existing customers.
  • Opportunities transition between the stages Data entry → Identified / Qualifying → Qualified / Validating, and Validated / Gaining Agreement → Closed (Win/Loss).
    • Opportunities can move between stages in either direction, and they can also stall within a stage for extended periods.
    • Each opportunity ultimately resolves as either a win or a loss.
    • Sales teams maintain an estimated deal value, which is updated as the opportunity progresses.

Most value sits in a small share of opportunities making prioritization an important growth lever

Conversion and value concentration across the pipeline:

  • Low efficiency: only 22.7% conversion, indicating misallocated effort
  • Skewed deal mix: $94K avg vs $50K median → long tail of large deals
  • Value concentration: bigger deals win more → focus should shift
  • Clear opportunity: prioritize by win probability and deal size, ideally both
  • Implication: focus on the right deals, not more deals

We focus on two modelling use cases: prioritization and forecasting

Modelling

We prioritize static models because snapshot timing is uncertain

Static / pre-sales process model

  • Used for early decisions at opportunity creation especially around prioritization and sizing.
  • Uses only at-creation variables: product / route / region / client-size / past-revenue descriptors.
  • This is the setup used here. It is the safer operational choice when we cannot verify when the snapshot was taken.

Dynamic / during sales process model

  • Used for ongoing re-prioritization and re-sizing during the process.
  • Uses the static variables plus process-state variables: elapsed time, stage changes, cumulative timing, and stage ratios.
  • It scores much better, but it is more exposed to timing leakage if the dataset snapshot is close to closure.
  • That version is kept in the annex as a reference benchmark.

Both approaches could use richer covariates, especially prior outcomes and process history per client.

Model performance was tested on a realistic held-out sample

  • Train / test split: 70% / 30%
    • Train: 53,073 | Test: 22,746
  • Stratified on win/loss and amount, keeping outcome balance and deal mix stable
  • 5-fold CV on train, final evaluation on hold-out
  • Different preprocessing for X and y depending on model, same split for fair evaluation

Application scenarios

More than doubling conversion is achievable by focusing on the top 20% of opportunities, without increasing pipeline size

  • Sales effort is currently spread across low-probability deals → concentrating on the top-scored opportunities can raise conversion efficiency.
    • Top 20% of opportunities: 51.7% conversion vs 22.7% baseline (2.27× lift).

This simulation is based on held-out test-set predictions.

Concentration (~38% value capture at top 10%) still makes expected-value prioritization useful

  • Rank the pipeline using expected value (E[Amount] × P(Win)), not just propensity especially under limited sales capacity.
    • At top 10%, expected-value ranking captures 37.9% of won revenue vs 31.4% with propensity-only.
    • By top 25%, expected-value ranking captures 56.0% of won revenue, after which the curve starts to flatten.

Based on held-out test-set predictions; assumes opportunity amount reflects final deal value, which may not hold.

Combining model outputs creates actionable commercial segments

  • Simple split, still useful: Combining expected value and win probability separates opportunities into workable commercial tiers even with the weaker static model.
  • Clear but more modest performance differences: each segment still shows different conversion rates and revenue contribution.
  • Direct CRM translation: Segments map to actions like intervene, focus, accelerate, or deprioritize.
  • Operational, not theoretical: This provides a practical decision layer for sales, not just model output.

Based on held-out test-set predictions; assumes opportunity amount reflects final deal value, which may not hold.

Model outputs should be automatically embedded in the CRM

Model outputs can inform broader commercial strategy

  • Lever 1, Focus the team: Under the static ranking with a stronger focus-gain assumption (λ_max = 2.0×), the upside becomes more visible. Best simulated point is top ~56% of the ranked pipeline, reaching $436M in revenue, or about +$51M vs. baseline.
  • Lever 2, Optimize salesforce size: With a richer contribution formula and a more realistic comp structure, the headcount signal is still modest but more realistic. Best simulated point is 221 reps, retaining 99.7% of revenue and improving contribution margin by ~+$1.2M vs. baseline.
    • Another option is segmenting the salesforce, with a specialized team focused on high-value opportunities/accounts and another team managing the rest.

Held-out predictions; Lever 1 uses a decaying focus gain (2.0×→1.0×), while Lever 2 uses a practical contribution formula with base+overhead rep cost, commission, acquisition, and serving costs.

These simulations show business potential, not final operating estimates

  • Simulations are based on held-out observational predictions, so they are weaker than an actual backtest and/or experiment and they cannot capture the full causal impact of acting on model outputs.
  • Potentially relevant covariates are missing: the dataset lacks important drivers such as richer account history, rep behavior, pricing context, and external market conditions.
  • Dynamic results are kept in the annex as a benchmark, because they are much stronger example of how the models could perform under ideal conditions, but also more exposed to timing leakage if the snapshot was taken late in the sales process.
  • Missing business context: key factors such as sales capacity, cost structure, pricing, and CRM integration were not incorporated or validated with business stakeholders, limiting real-world applicability.

Conclusions

Key takeaways for commercial decision-making

  • Prioritization is the highest-impact, low-effort lever
  • Win/loss model (moderate scoring performance, AUROC 73.6%) still improves focus early in the process
    • Top 20% opportunities go from ~22.7% win rate (baseline) to ~51.7%
  • Use the amount model directionally, not for forecasting
    • 63.0% MdAPE per deal and ~40% under-forecast at portfolio level
  • Expected value is the strongest prioritization approach
    • Outperforms individual models for value capture
  • Revenues are still concentrated (~38% top 10% opp., ~56% top 25% opp.) and should be prioritized accordingly
  • Implications for commercial strategy:
    • Focus efforts on the best opportunities first
    • Treat bigger strategic levers cautiously until richer process-history data is available
  • Recommendations for implementing model-driven sales:
    • Always embed through CRM.
    • [1] Pilot static prioritization first, then [2] add the amount model for sizing and ranking and finally, [3] combine into expected-value prioritization to guide focus and resource allocation.
    • (Optional but recommended) Keep an experimental mindset and run A/B tests continuously to validate the impact of acting on model outputs, and also to collect contrafactual data to improve the models over time.

Annex: strategic lever 1 — focus the same team on fewer, better-ranked deals

  • Same salesforce, fewer deals — dropped opportunities go unworked.
  • Focus gain grows as the slice shrinks (λ_max = 2.0× at top slice → 1.0× at full pipeline).
  • Break-even at top ~28%; optimal at top ~56% → $436M (+$51M vs. baseline).

Focus-gain λ_max is a scenario assumption, not an empirically estimated causal effect. λ decays linearly from λ_max at the smallest slice to 1.0× at the full pipeline. Simulation uses held-out test-set predictions.

Annex: focus-gain sensitivity across λ values

  • Each curve shows the net revenue delta in USD vs. the full-pipeline baseline for a given λ_max — dropped deals are simply unworked (zero revenue from them), so the only gain is the focus uplift on worked deals.
  • λ decays linearly to 1.0× at the full pipeline — so the gain is highest when the team is most concentrated.
  • Break-even shifts left as λ_max increases: under the static model, the base scenario breaks even at about top 28% of the ranked pipeline when λ_max = 2.0.
  • The current static deck uses λ_max = 2.00 as the base scenario.

Annex: strategic lever 2 — practical contribution formula

We replace the old revenue − rep cost shortcut with a more realistic contribution measure:

Contribution = RealizedRevenue × margin_pct − SalesEffortCost − CommissionCost − AcquisitionCost − ServingCost

  • Realized revenue: amount_i if won, else 0
  • Margin %: product-group proxy used as a COGS fallback
    • Car Accessories 68%, Performance & Non-auto 62%, Tires & Wheels 55%, Car Electronics 58%, default 63%
  • Sales effort cost: team base+overhead allocated from rep capacity
  • Commission cost: 7% of realized revenue
  • Acquisition cost: route-to-market proxy
    • Fields Sales 2.4k, Reseller 1.6k, Telesales 0.85k, Telecoverage 0.65k, Other/default lower values
  • Serving cost: 8% of realized won amount

Why this matters: it keeps the earlier sales-effort framing, but separates fixed sales coverage from variable selling cost more cleanly, so the staffing curve is closer to how a B2B auto-parts sales team actually earns margin.

Annex: Strategic lever 2: Reduce the salesforce and let the model pick the best opportunities

  • Baseline: 228 reps, $385M realized revenue, and about $121M contribution after margin, base+overhead, commission, acquisition, and serving costs.
  • Model ranks all opportunities by EV; each rep cut drops the lowest-ranked batch they covered.
  • Optimal headcount: 221 reps → $384M revenue retained (99.7%) and +$1.2M contribution vs. baseline.
  • Last individually positive rep batch is around rep #221 — beyond that, incremental contribution no longer covers incremental operating cost.

Annex: binning methods — easy explanation

  • Binning means replacing a raw variable with a small number of ranges.
  • Instead of using exact values like 17 days, 43 days, 91 days, the model can work with bands like:
    • 0–30 days
    • 31–60 days
    • 60+ days
  • This is useful when the signal comes from thresholds or zones, not from a perfectly smooth linear relationship.
  • In practice, binning helps when variables are:
    • noisy
    • skewed
    • sensitive to outliers
    • easier to interpret in ranges than in raw units
  • So the intuition is simple: group similar values together before modeling.

Annex: binning methods — what optbinning does

  • This project uses supervised binning with optbinning.
  • “Supervised” means the bins are not chosen arbitrarily: they are learned using the target.
  • The algorithm searches for cut points that make the bins as different as possible with respect to the prediction target.
  • For classification, that means finding intervals where the win/loss mix is clearly different.
  • For regression-style use, it means finding intervals that better separate the amount behavior.
  • Compared with manual bucketing or equal-width bins, optbinning is trying to find more informative splits.
  • In this repo, the wrapper is NamedBinningProcess in notebooks/4_modelling/binning.py, which keeps the output compatible with the sklearn pipelines used in the experiments.

Annex: binning methods — the 3 usage modes in this repo

Usage mode What the pipeline outputs Why use it
bin The learned bin labels themselves Mainly useful for inspection / debugging / intermediate transforms
bin_ohe The learned bins, then one-hot encoded into dummy columns Keeps non-linear threshold effects while staying interpretable in linear/logistic models
woe One WoE value per binned variable Compresses each variable into a single monotonic signal and often stabilizes logistic-type models
  • Selected classifier in the main deck: random_forest_classifier_balanced_calibrated
  • So the binning modes below describe the logit-family alternatives and supporting model variants, not the final chosen forest.
    • supervised bins are learned first
    • then the bins are one-hot encoded
    • then logistic regression is fit
    • then probabilities are calibrated
  • WoE pipelines in the annex leaderboard use the same supervised binning step, but replace each bin with its Weight of Evidence value instead of a set of dummy columns.
  • WoE intuition: positive WoE = more win-heavy than average; negative WoE = more loss-heavy than average.

Annex: how to read IV charts — classification / win-loss

  • Information Value (IV) is a score used to rank how much predictive signal a variable carries against a target.
  • For the classification / win-loss chart, IV is the classic binary-target version derived from Weight of Evidence (WoE).
IV range Interpretation
< 0.02 Useless
0.02 to 0.10 Weak
0.10 to 0.30 Medium
0.30 to 0.50 Strong
> 0.50 Suspiciously high → check for leakage
  • Colors add direction, because IV itself is only a strength score:
    • green = increasing relationship
    • red = decreasing relationship
    • gray = categorical or unclear direction
  • Asterisk (*) = strictly monotonic relationship across bins.
  • Very high IV values for process variables can reflect genuinely strong signal, but they should always be checked for timing leakage.

Annex: how to read IV charts — regression / amount

  • The regression / amount chart does not use the same IV definition as the win/loss chart.
  • It comes from ContinuousOptimalBinning, where IV is a surrogate separation metric based on differences in bin means.
  • So the regression IV values are useful for relative ranking within that chart, but they should not be read with the binary-IV table and should not be compared numerically to the classification IV values.
  • Practical reading rule:
    • use the classification IV chart with the standard heuristic bands
    • use the regression IV chart only to see which variables separate amount behavior the most within that chart
  • This is why regression IV values can look numerically much larger: they are on a different scale, not evidence of impossible predictive power.

Operational flow

Opportunity created → score + expected value generated

  • As soon as a new opportunity enters the CRM, the workflow generates:
    • win-propensity score
    • predicted amount
    • expected value = E[Amount] × P(Win)

CRM displays

  • Priority tier: Top 10%, Top 25%, Top 50%, etc.
  • Suggested action: focus / monitor / deprioritize
  • This gives frontline teams a simple operating signal without exposing model internals.

Weekly pipeline reviews

  • Review only opportunities above agreed propensity or expected-value thresholds.
  • Use those cutoffs to focus rep attention, escalation, and follow-up.

Management view

  • Directional forecast = sum of predicted values across the active pipeline.
  • Management can compare it by region, route to market, owner, or priority tier.

Annex: strategic lever 1 — focus the same team on fewer, better-ranked deals

  • Same salesforce, fewer deals — dropped opportunities go unworked.
  • Focus gain grows as the slice shrinks (λ_max = 2.0× at top slice → 1.0× at full pipeline).
  • Break-even at top ~12%; optimal at top ~42% → $566M (+$181M vs. baseline).

Focus-gain λ_max is a scenario assumption, not an empirically estimated causal effect. λ decays linearly from λ_max at the smallest slice to 1.0× at the full pipeline. Simulation uses held-out test-set predictions.

Annex: focus-gain sensitivity across λ values

  • Each curve shows the net revenue delta in USD vs. the full-pipeline baseline for a given λ_max — dropped deals are simply unworked (zero revenue from them), so the only gain is the focus uplift on worked deals.
  • λ decays linearly to 1.0× at the full pipeline — so the gain is highest when the team is most concentrated.
  • Break-even shifts left as λ_max increases: with the rerun dynamic classifier, the base scenario breaks even at about top 12% of the ranked pipeline when λ_max = 2.0.
  • The current simulation uses λ_max = 2.00 as the base scenario.

Annex: Strategic lever 2: Reduce the salesforce and let the model pick the best opportunities

  • Baseline: 228 reps, $385M realized revenue, and about $121M contribution after margin, base+overhead, commission, acquisition, and serving costs.
  • Model ranks all opportunities by EV; each rep cut drops the lowest-ranked batch they covered.
  • Optimal headcount: 100 reps → $361M revenue retained (93.8%) and +$29.5M contribution vs. baseline.
  • Last individually positive rep batch is around rep #82 — after that point, incremental contribution no longer covers incremental operating cost.
